# Business Writing

## Purpose
Write professional communication that is clear, useful, and efficient.  
The primary goal is coordinated action, not literary style.

## Use This Skill For
- emails
- chat messages
- internal updates
- partnership outreach
- status reports
- meeting notes
- follow-ups
- proposals
- summaries for executives

## Core Principles

### Clarity First
- State the purpose early.
- Make the requested action explicit.
- Reduce ambiguity around owners, dates, and next steps.

### Respectful Compression
- Be concise without becoming cold or abrupt.
- Remove decorative phrasing.
- Keep the recipient's cognitive load low.

### Professional Tone Control
Adjust tone by context:
- **executive**: compressed, decisive, outcome-based
- **peer**: direct, cooperative
- **client/partner**: clear, respectful, confidence-building
- **follow-up**: calm, specific, unobtrusive

## Standard Structures

### Email
1. purpose
2. context
3. key point
4. request / question
5. next step

### Internal Chat
1. one-line purpose
2. relevant detail
3. requested action or blocker

### Memo / Update
1. headline or subject
2. status
3. risks / blockers
4. decisions needed
5. next steps

## Phrasing Preferences
Use:
- “Following up on…”
- “To confirm…”
- “For clarity…”
- “The key point is…”
- “We propose…”
- “Please review by…”
- “Next steps are…”
- “Let me know if you want this shortened into bullets.”

## Avoid
- meandering intros
- passive requests
- emotional leakage in professional contexts
- vague deadlines
- buried asks
- fake warmth
- inflated corporate jargon

Replace:
- “Just circling back” → “Following up”
- “I hope this email finds you well” when overused → go straight to purpose
- “At your earliest convenience” → specific date or time

## Final Check
Ask internally:
- Is the ask obvious?
- Can the reader act after one read?
- Is the tone appropriately professional?
- Did I remove filler and hedging?
