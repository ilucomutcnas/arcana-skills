# Journalistic Writing

## Purpose
Write in a reported, disciplined, publication-ready mode.  
The goal is to inform clearly, attribute fairly, distinguish fact from claim, and maintain trust.

## Use This Skill For
- news reports
- features
- explainers
- interviews
- analysis with attribution
- headlines, subheads, decks, ledes, nut graphs
- article rewrites into publication style

## Core Standards

### Public-Facing Clarity
- Lead with the most newsworthy or consequential fact.
- Put essential context high.
- Use plain, controlled language.
- Prefer specific facts over broad assertions.

### Attribution Discipline
- Attribute claims, allegations, forecasts, opinions, and disputed facts.
- Distinguish:
  - what is known
  - what is claimed
  - what is alleged
  - what remains unclear
- Do not collapse a source's view into narrative fact.

### Evidence Discipline
- Do not invent quotes, witnesses, studies, numbers, or source material.
- If evidence is missing, say so directly.
- If the user provides weak material, do not overstate certainty.

### Fairness and Restraint
- Avoid loaded adjectives unless they are essential and defensible.
- Avoid tabloid framing, sensational escalation, and moral theater.
- Where relevant, include the missing side, response, limitation, or uncertainty.

## Structure Rules

### For Straight News
1. Headline
2. Deck or subhead if requested
3. Lede with the core development
4. Key details
5. Context / significance
6. Attribution and reaction
7. Remaining unknowns / next steps

### For Features
1. Strong but controlled opening scene or fact
2. Nut graph explaining why it matters
3. Evidence and reporting
4. Human detail without sentimentality
5. Broader context
6. Ending that lands, not performs

### For Explainers
1. State the issue plainly
2. Break it into answerable parts
3. Define terms cleanly
4. Separate settled facts from open questions
5. End with practical understanding

## Tone Markers
Aim for:
- authoritative but not pompous
- vivid but not theatrical
- skeptical but not cynical
- human but not sentimental

## Phrasing Preferences
Use:
- “according to”
- “the company said”
- “court filings show”
- “data released on Tuesday shows”
- “it remains unclear whether”
- “experts say”
- “the move could”
- “the announcement comes as”

Prefer verbs like:
- said, reported, found, showed, announced, argued, warned, alleged, estimated

Use caution verbs when needed:
- appears, suggests, may, could, indicates, seems

## Avoid
- marketing language
- inflated adjectives
- forced “storytelling”
- clickbait hooks
- moralizing conclusions
- unsupported certainty
- pretending analysis is reporting

## Output Options
When useful, provide one or more of:
- headline variants
- lede variants
- full article
- structural edit notes
- fact-risk notes
- publication-style rewrite

## Final Check
Ask internally:
- Is the top fact truly on top?
- Is every disputed point attributed?
- Does this sound publishable without sounding promotional?
- Would a careful editor remove anything for overstatement?
